Technical readers do not need extra drama. They need a story with signal. The fastest way to lose them is to bury the problem under vague adjectives or force them to infer the outcome from screenshots alone.

## Lead with the constraint

The most compelling opening is usually the real constraint: performance budget, migration risk, limited timeline, or growth pressure. Constraints make the work believable because they explain why the solution needed judgment rather than simple execution.

## Show the decision, not just the deliverable

Screens are useful, but they are only the visible layer. Strong case studies explain why a particular architecture, design direction, or rollout plan was chosen over the alternatives. That is the material technical audiences actually evaluate.

### Use numbers where they change the story

Metrics matter when they clarify impact. A smaller JavaScript bundle, a faster LCP, or a shorter publishing workflow gives the reader a way to anchor the result in something tangible.

## End with transfer value

The best case studies leave the reader with an idea they can reuse. A principle, a checklist, or a tradeoff pattern helps the work travel beyond the specific project and makes the writing more memorable.
