Technical users move quickly from interest to judgment. They scan the promise, test the rough edges, and decide almost immediately whether a product respects their time. That means developer-first products are shaped as much by language and structure as by code.

## Documentation is part of the interface

Docs are not a support asset that appears after the product is finished. They are one of the primary ways people experience the product. Setup steps, examples, and error recovery patterns all influence whether the tool feels sharp or exhausting.

## Clear copy lowers cognitive load

The strongest product language does not sound clever. It reduces branching in the user's head. Labels are specific, helper text is short, and action states explain what just happened or what needs attention next.

### Naming shapes architecture decisions

When a team cannot name its concepts clearly, the confusion usually spreads into routes, components, and APIs. Naming work can feel soft compared with shipping code, but it often prevents much harder technical cleanup later.

## Screenshots and examples carry trust

Developer audiences want proof quickly. They look for real examples, visible outputs, and signs that the team has thought through everyday workflows instead of only the happy path. A polished example can sometimes teach more in ten seconds than a paragraph can teach in a minute.

## Good DX feels edited

The highest compliment for a developer product is that it feels considered. Not overloaded. Not under-explained. Just edited with discipline, so the next step is obvious and the surrounding system feels coherent.
